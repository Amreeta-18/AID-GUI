    Course Description

    This class will give you hands-on experience with usability evaluation and user-centered design. In this class you will not learn how to implement user interfaces, but rather how to design these based on the needs of users, which you will determine, and learn how to evaluate your designs rigorously. This is a class for those who wish to know more about usability, human-computer interaction, the psychological aspects of computing, evaluation, and/or experimentation.

    There will be no programming. However, a significant portion of your grade will be based on a team project, where you will propose, prototype (using a special prototyping tool), and evaluate your own solutions.

    Learning Objectives

    At the completion of this course, students will be able to:

    1. Describe the human centered design process and usability engineering process and their roles in system design and development.
    2. Discuss usability design guidelines, their foundations, assumptions, advantages, and weaknesses.
    3. Describe basics of human subjects research.
    4. Complete a basic human subjects research certification form.
    5. Design a user interface based on analysis of human needs and prepare a prototype system.
    6. Assess user interfaces using different usability engineering techniques.
    7. Make an oral presentation that justifies design decisions.

    What to bring to class

    • Laptops/tablets will be useful for some activities, but if bringing a laptop/tablet isn't ideal for you, you can make notes on paper/phone and then catch up via your laptop/tablet later if needed.
    • Paper and pen or pencil to write with will be needed for some activities. Please always bring them. (Or a tablet computer works fine for these too.)
    • Bring your Tenets & Traps cards.

    How the course will be conducted, method of instruction

    Hands-on style: There is some lecture, but this class also has a lot hands-on work that is conducted in person. You'll do some work individually, but much of it will be work on group projects (~4 person groups).

    Participation: This class will be very interactive with lots of hands-on activities.  Active participation is part of your grade.  Although some contributions can be made asynchronously, you won't have the information you need to make them if you haven't been paying attention to the activities in class.

    Responsibilities: You're responsible for having done the reading before attending class that day, which will help you participate in the discussion. To help everyone keep up with the reading, there will be a short quiz almost every week. I may not go over all the reading material in class, preferring to spend that time elaborating or discussing that material with you. But this doesn't mean the assigned reading isn't important or won't be covered in a test.

    About your phone: If you can't resist reading/texting on your phone, please sit in the back, as this is distracting to others.

    About multitasking: You're a human, and humans pay a huge cognitive "tax" when we try to multitask -- as you'll learn in class. If you find yourself trying to keep an eye on your emails and messages during class, know that you'll be better off as a student and a future professional if you learn to wait till after class to check your phone/etc.

    Deadlines

    • Most Homeworks and Project Assignments will have an automatic 2-day deadline extension.  Most other assignments/activites will not.
      • The automatic 2-day extension (HWs and Project Assignments only): Occasionally unforeseen things happen that make it impossible to meet a deadline.  A teammate might suddenly become ill and the rest of the team needs to figure out how to get the work done without them; your internet/power might go down for a day; we might have a snow day; etc.  To accommodate these extenuating circumstances, we will automatically accept any HW or Project Assignment 48 hours late, (unless otherwise announced).  No penalty, no emails are necessary.  No other extensions are possible.

    COVID Planning

      • Here are instructions for what else you should do.
    • If COVID becomes a bigger issue than just a few individuals, we may have to temporarily switch the entire class to zoom. In that case, I'll let everyone know via Canvas Announcements and through the class email list.
    • In the case of needing to zoom with someone sick or isolating, those lectures will also be recorded and posted on Canvas.  Other classes will not be recorded.

    Grading

    I have high expectations. Thus, in this class, "A" does not mean "adequate" or "nothing really wrong": instead, it means "excellent". For an A, you should expect to work hard and get the most you can out of the class.

    Weights:

    • Participation: 5%
    • Homeworks and quizzes (but we'll drop your lowest quiz): 15%
    • Midterm: 25%
    • Final exam: 30%
    • Project (group): 25%.
      • This comes from:
        • Proposal: 10%
        • Formative research into your users and their tasks: 20%
        • Prototype: 35% (10% as of first design gallery, 25% at end)
        • Evaluation plan: 10%
        • Evaluation: 25%
        • Presentation: (this is part of prototype scores)
      • Grading note: this is a large class, and we have only 1 TA. 
        • Thus, not all parts of the group projects can be formally graded.  (You won't know in advance which parts can be and which can't be.) For a project installment not graded, the weight will be distributed equally among all other project installments.
        • In those cases, we'll all informally "grade" portions of that installment together in class, so that everyone can see feedback useful to the upcoming project installments.
      • Problematic contributions to team projects: The team receives one grade for every project handin. However, this will change if team members do not contribute relatively equally to the effort. If your team contributions warrant adjustments to your course grade, these adjustments will happen at the very end of the term. In past terms, a few students' course grades have changed by as much as a whole letter grade from these adjustments, so it's wise to keep up with your contributions to your team's efforts throughout the term. 

    Classroom Policies

    This class is our community.

    Every student should feel safe and welcome to contribute in this course, and it is all of our jobs to make sure this is the case. I will try to establish this tone whenever possible, but ultimately the responsibility for cultivating a safe and welcoming community belongs to the studentsâ€”that means you! Fortunately, forming a safe and welcoming community is not too hard. A good place to start is to recognize (and continually remind yourself) of the following facts:

    • Your classmates come from a variety of cultural, economic, and educational backgrounds. Something that is obvious to you may not be obvious to them, and vice versa.
    • Your classmates are human beings with intelligence and emotions. This applies even when one or the other of you is posting anonymously. Rudeness and disrespect are unprofessional, and have no place in this course or in your career.
    • Your classmates are here to learn. They have the right to pursue their education without being distracted by others' disruptive behavior, or made uncomfortable by inappropriate jokes or unwanted sexual interest.

    In short, treat your classmates as respected colleagues, support each other when needed, have fun without spoiling it for anyone else, and everybody wins.
